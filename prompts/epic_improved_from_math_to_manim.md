Create a 75-second cinematic Manim animation titled “Linear vs Nonlinear Systems: When Small Causes Stay Small, and When They Explode.”

Audience: high school physics students.

Start with two side-by-side systems. On the left, show a linear spring-mass oscillator with Hooke’s law \(F=-kx\), smooth sinusoidal motion, and proportional response: doubling the initial displacement doubles the amplitude. On the right, show a nonlinear driven pendulum or logistic map where tiny changes in initial conditions grow into visibly different futures.

Use clear, fully formatted LaTeX equations:
\[
F=-kx
\]
\[
x(t)=A\cos(\omega t+\phi)
\]
\[
x_{n+1}=rx_n(1-x_n)
\]
\[
|\Delta x(t)|\approx |\Delta x(0)|e^{\lambda t}
\]

Animate two nearly identical initial conditions in each system. In the linear case, keep trajectories close and predictable. In the nonlinear case, show them separating, branching, and forming a chaotic pattern. Use color coding: blue for linear stability, orange/red for nonlinear sensitivity. End with the takeaway: “Linear systems scale. Nonlinear systems can surprise.”
