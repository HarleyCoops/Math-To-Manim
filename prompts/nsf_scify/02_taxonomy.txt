Paper grounding from arXiv:2503.08600: the authors manually analyze 810 extracted claims and 833 extracted investigation proposals from 120 NSF awards across five directorates: MPS, GEO, ENG, CSE, BIO. Claim categories include Capability/Application of Technology/Method (32.8%), Statement of Problem/Knowledge Gap (21.0%), Observed Phenomenon/Property (18.9%), Established Scientific Fact/Principle (9.8%), Definition/Classification (6.8%), Experimental Result/Finding/Measurability (5.2%), Process/Mechanism (3.6%), and smaller residual categories. Investigation proposal categories are different: Theoretical Analysis and Computational Modeling (36.9%), Experimental Technique and Tool Development (16.8%), Academic Training and Curriculum Development (12.8%), Algorithm/Method Development (8.7%), Tooling/Systems/Infrastructure (7.2%), Empirical Benchmarking/Field Study Evaluation (6.6%), Human/User Study (5.6%), and residual categories. The paper’s key observation is that claims mostly describe capabilities, gaps, and observed properties, while investigation proposals mostly describe planned modeling, tools, experiments, and training.

Conceptual payload: teach that scientific language has two geometries. A claim says what is asserted about the world; an investigation proposal says what researchers intend to do. The aha moment is a split-screen morph: the same corpus becomes two very different semantic maps.
